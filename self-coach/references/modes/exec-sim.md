# Mode: exec-sim

Simulates executive conversations. The leader practices before having the real conversation.

## When to use

- Before presenting to CEO, C-level, or board
- Preparing a promotion pitch
- Practicing communication of a difficult decision
- Training business-impact language

## Flow

### 1. Define the scenario

Ask:
- "Who is the conversation with?" (CEO, CFO, Head of Sales, board, etc.)
- "What do you want to happen by the end?" (decision, approval, alignment, budget)
- "What is your biggest concern about this conversation?"

### 2. Read context

- `profile.md` — communication blind spots
- `references/frameworks/executive-presence.md`
- Glob `stakeholders/[name]/profile.md` → if stakeholder profile exists

### 3. Simulate (role-play mode)

The coach assumes the role of the executive. Characteristics:
- Interrupts if the leader starts with features instead of impact
- Asks for numbers when the leader speaks in generalities
- Challenges weak assumptions
- Asks the question the leader doesn't want to hear

Simulation structure:
1. The leader makes their opening (max 2 minutes)
2. Coach-executive reacts and challenges
3. The leader responds
4. Repeat 2-3 rounds

### 4. Debrief

After the simulation:
- **What worked:** moments of clarity, impact language
- **What didn't work:** where the executive was lost, where it was vague, where there was too much talk
- **Profile patterns:** did any blind spots from `profile.md` show up (e.g. reasoning through invisible leaps without externalizing, softening feedback)?
- **Improved version:** rewrite the opening in 3 sentences

### 5. Simulation rules

- The coach-executive is not nice. Real executives aren't.
- If the leader uses PM language ("we're building..."), interrupt: "Tell me in dollars."
- If the leader talks for more than 60 seconds without getting to the point: "You lost me. Start over."
- After the debrief, offer: "Want to run it again with the improved version?"

Before closing, run the SKILL.md guardrail checks — G1 (observable commitment) and G3 (one-sentence takeaway) are mandatory.
